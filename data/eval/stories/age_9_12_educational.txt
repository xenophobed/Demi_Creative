In 1928, Alexander Fleming returned from vacation to find mold growing on his bacterial cultures. Most scientists would have thrown the contaminated dishes away. Instead, Fleming noticed something remarkable: the bacteria around the mold had died. That accidental observation led to the discovery of penicillin, which has since saved an estimated two hundred million lives. The lesson is powerful — mistakes and accidents are not always failures. Sometimes the most important discoveries come from paying attention to things that go wrong rather than ignoring them.
